1. Determining the Applicable Tax Structure
Grantor Trust (Most Common): Most MAPTs and many FDTs are structured so the grantor (the person who created the trust) remains responsible for taxes. In this case, rental income is reported on your personal tax return (Form 1040) using the progressive individual rates.
Non-Grantor Trust: If the trust is a separate taxable entity, it files its own return (Form 1041). Any income it retains is taxed at highly compressed "trust rates" that hit the top bracket very quickly.

No, a non-grantor irrevocable trust filing Form 1041 (and Oregon Form OR-41) cannot use a standard deduction. Unlike individual taxpayers, trusts are strictly limited to deducting specific expenses actually incurred.
Deduction Rules for Trusts (Form 1041 / OR-41)
No Standard Deduction: Trusts and estates do not have a standard deduction. They must "itemize" every expense on the return.
Personal Exemption: In lieu of a standard deduction, a trust receives a small statutory exemption:
Complex Trust (typically a MAPT that accumulates income): $100
Simple Trust (required to distribute all income annually): $300
Estate: $600
Rental Property Deductions: Because the rental is a business activity, the trust deducts all direct property expenses (mortgage interest, property taxes, repairs, and depreciation) on Schedule E before the net income even reaches the trust's main tax calculation page.
Administrative Deductions: The trust can also deduct costs necessary for its administration, such as trustee fees, legal fees, and tax preparation costs.

1. During Your Lifetime (The "Burn")
As the grantor, you rent the property and claim depreciation on your personal 1040.

2. At Your Death (The "Reset")
Because you kept a Limited Power of Appointment, the property is included in your estate for tax purposes. This triggers the Step-Up in Basis under IRC § 1014.
The IRS ignores your $300k adjusted basis.
The IRS ignores the $200k in depreciation you "burned" through.
The basis is reset to the Fair Market Value (FMV) on the date of your death.
Example: If the house is worth $900k when you die, your child’s new basis is $900k. The $200k in prior depreciation vanishes and is never taxed.
3. For Your Disabled Child (The "Clean Slate")
Your child (or their Special Needs Trust) starts fresh.
If they sell it for $900k the next day: $0 Capital Gains Tax.
If they keep it as a rental: They start a brand new 27.5-year depreciation schedule based on the $900k value.

If you use a Non-Grantor Trust to "sequester" the tax, you usually lose the step-up.
In that case, your child inherits your $300k basis.
If they sell it for $900k, they owe tax on the $600k gain, plus a 25% depreciation recapture tax on that $200k you claimed while alive.
